# AI as co-thinker

The pattern of treating an LLM as a thinking partner you invite in
from the start of a task, rather than a tool you reach for after
you're already stuck.

## Key sources

- Mollick, *Co-Intelligence* (2024) — "always invite AI to the
  table." See [[sources/reading/2026-05-15-mollick-co-intelligence]].
- Karpathy, *Software 3.0 talk* (June 2025) — frames the LLM as the
  partial-autonomy partner ranging from autocomplete to fully
  agentic. See [[sources/reading/2026-04-02-karpathy-software-3]].
- My own working log of doing it — pairing on the migration design
  with the dial set per-subtask. See
  [[sources/journal/2026-06-05-ai-pairing-at-work]].

## My open questions

- Does this work for purely creative tasks (drafting fiction,
  composing music) the same way it works for analytical ones?
  [[records/people/sam-reyes]] is feeling the same tension on the
  writing side — we talked it through on the 06-05 work day (see
  [[sources/journal/2026-06-05-ai-pairing-at-work]]): the draft comes
  easier with the LLM, but you have to fight to keep your own voice.
- Where does "co-thinker" end and "co-author" begin? Attribution?
- When it's always there, am I outsourcing the struggle that used to
  build my intuition? The re-derivation tax (06-05 entry) is my
  current answer, but it depends on me actually paying it.

## Practice

I default to opening the chat alongside any thinking task that
isn't strictly mechanical. The cost of inviting is zero; the
ceiling is high. The skill is setting the autonomy slider per
subtask — low where I have taste, high where I don't.

There's a quiet rhyme with [[records/themes/tools-for-thought]]: the
slip-box and the LLM are both partners that think *with* me by being
asked good questions, not by handing me answers.
