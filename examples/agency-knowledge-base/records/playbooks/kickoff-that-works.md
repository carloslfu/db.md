# A kickoff that works

The first meeting sets the whole engagement. A good kickoff trades vague
enthusiasm for four concrete agreements, so nobody is guessing in week
three. This is the pattern, abstracted across engagements.

## Land the brief in one shared document

Before anything creative starts, there is one brief everyone has read:
goals, scope (and explicitly what is *out* of scope), deliverables, and
the deadline that actually matters. Out-of-scope is not an afterthought —
naming it on day one is what makes the scope-creep conversation easy
later (see [[records/playbooks/scope-creep-handling]]). If the client
hasn't signed off on the brief, the kickoff isn't done.

## Lock the voice and the decision-maker

Capture two things in writing at kickoff:

1. **The voice.** How does this client sound — formal or casual, what
   words are banned, who are their people called? Every draft you produce
   afterward matches it. A startup and an enterprise want opposite
   registers; get it explicit so revisions aren't about taste.
2. **The decision-maker.** Exactly one person gives final sign-off on
   each deliverable type. "The team will review" is how a project dies by
   committee. Name the approver and the path.

## Commit to a concrete first deliverable

Leave the kickoff with one specific, near-term artifact on the calendar —
a first layout, a concept round, a draft section — with a date. A concrete
first deliverable converts goodwill into momentum and gives the client
something real to react to while the relationship is still forming.
Abstract "next steps" stall; a dated artifact moves.

## Set the review cadence on day one

Agree how often you'll show work and who joins. A regular rhythm
(roughly weekly for an active build) keeps surprises small and feedback
cheap — a problem caught at the next review costs a revision, the same
problem caught at delivery costs the deadline. Put the first few reviews
on the calendar before you leave the call.

## Why this works

Kickoffs fail quietly: everyone leaves happy, then scope, voice,
approver, and cadence each get rediscovered the expensive way. Pinning
all four up front turns the rest of the engagement into execution against
agreements instead of a running negotiation. The thirty extra minutes at
the start buys back days later. And plan the exit while you plan the entry:
when the work ships, close the engagement as deliberately as you opened it
— see [[records/playbooks/client-handoff]].
