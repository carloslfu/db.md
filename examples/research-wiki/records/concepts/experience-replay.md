# Experience replay

Experience replay is the technique of storing an agent's transitions
(state, action, reward, next state) in a buffer and training on random
minibatches sampled from that buffer, rather than on consecutive
transitions as they arrive. It is one of the two ingredients that made deep
[[records/concepts/q-learning]] stable in DQN
([[records/papers/mnih-dqn-2015]]).

## Two problems it solves

1. **Correlation.** Consecutive transitions in an episode are highly
   correlated, and training a neural network on a stream of correlated
   samples violates the i.i.d. assumption stochastic gradient descent
   relies on, causing oscillation and divergence. Sampling uniformly at
   random from a large buffer breaks that temporal correlation.
2. **Sample efficiency.** Online RL would use each transition once and throw
   it away. A replay buffer lets each transition be reused in many gradient
   updates, which matters enormously when environment interaction is the
   expensive resource.

## Why it pairs with off-policy learning

Replay only works with an **off-policy** algorithm, because the transitions
in the buffer were generated by older versions of the policy. Q-learning's
`max`-based target ([[records/concepts/q-learning]]) is off-policy by
construction, so it can learn correctly from stale data — which is exactly
why DQN is value-based rather than a policy gradient. On-policy methods like
[[records/concepts/policy-gradient]] and [[records/concepts/actor-critic]]
cannot reuse a buffer this way; A3C ([[records/papers/mnih-a3c-2016]])
reaches for **parallel actors** instead to get the same decorrelation
benefit on fresh on-policy data.

## Relationship to the deadly triad

Replay is off-policy, which is one leg of the **deadly triad** (see
[[records/concepts/function-approximation]]). It is therefore a double-edged
tool: it stabilizes training by decorrelating samples, but the off-policy
distribution shift it introduces is one of the very ingredients that makes
deep value learning unstable. DQN's second stabilizer, the frozen target
network, is what keeps the combination in check.

## Variants

- **Prioritized replay** samples high-TD-error transitions more often, on
  the intuition that surprising transitions carry more to learn.
- **Hindsight replay** relabels failed trajectories with the goals they did
  reach, manufacturing reward signal in sparse-reward goal-conditioned
  tasks.

## Open questions

- *Buffer staleness:* a very large buffer holds transitions from policies
  far from the current one, worsening off-policy bias; sizing it trades
  decorrelation against staleness.
- *Priority bias:* non-uniform sampling skews the gradient and needs
  importance-sampling correction to stay unbiased.
